# Wireframing Best Practices
## Summary

Wireframing is a crucial step in the UI/UX design process that focuses on establishing the structure, hierarchy, and user flows of an interface before moving to visual design. Effective wireframing emphasizes low-fidelity sketches to rapidly explore ideas, validate concepts, and communicate design intent without getting bogged down in visual details.

## Key Takeaways
- Begin with paper or whiteboard sketches to encourage rapid ideation and avoid premature focus on aesthetics
- Use consistent visual conventions (rectangles for images, lines for text, basic shapes for interactive elements)
- Focus on structure, information hierarchy, and user flows rather than colors, typography, or imagery
- Design for actual device constraints: 44px minimum touch targets on mobile, thumb-friendly placement, responsive breakpoints for web
- Annotate wireframes clearly to explain functionality, interactions, and edge cases for developers and stakeholders
- Embrace iteration: create multiple versions, gather feedback early, and refine based on usability testing
- Choose appropriate fidelity level: low-fidelity for exploration and validation, mid-fidelity for detailed layout, high-fidelity for final specification
- Test wireframes with real users to validate information architecture and task flows before investing in visual design

## Origins/History
Wireframing evolved from architectural blueprints and engineering schematics as a way to plan interface layouts. The practice became formalized in the early days of web design as teams needed to communicate complex interactions and content structures before developing visual designs.

## Applications
- Early-stage design exploration and concept validation
- Information architecture planning and testing
- Communication of layout and structure to cross-functional teams
- Usability testing of navigation and task flows
- Specification documentation for development handoff
- Collaborative design workshops and sketching sessions
