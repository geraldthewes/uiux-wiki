# Miller's Law

The average person can only keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory.

## Overview
Also known as "The Magical Number Seven, Plus or Minus Two," this principle describes the limit of short-term memory capacity.

## UX Applications
- Navigation menus: Limit to 5-9 top-level items
- Forms: Group related fields in chunks of 5-9
- Lists: Break long lists into smaller, scannable groups
- Dashboard widgets: Limit visible widgets to 5-9
- Steps in a process: Keep to 5-9 steps where possible
- Tabbed interfaces: Limit tabs to 5-9 visible options
- Permission systems: Group permissions in logical chunks

## Design Implications
1. **Chunking**: Break information into meaningful groups of 5-9 items
2. **Progressive Disclosure**: Show only essential options, hide advanced ones
3. **Visual Grouping**: Use spacing, borders, or backgrounds to create chunks
4. **Prioritization**: Display most important items first within chunks
5. **Search & Filtering**: Provide ways to reduce large sets to manageable chunks
6. **Pagination**: Break long lists into pages of 5-9 items
7. **Memory Aids**: Use visual cues to reduce memory load

## Related Concepts
- **Hick's Law**: Decision time increases with number of choices
- **Cognitive Load**: Total mental effort required to process information
- **Working Memory**: System that temporarily holds and manipulates information
- **Garbage Collector Principle**: Design for recognition over recall
