# Typography Basics

## Summary
Typography is the art and technique of arranging type to make written language legible, readable, and appealing when displayed. It involves selecting typefaces, point sizes, line lengths, line-spacing (leading), and letter-spacing (tracking and kerning).

## Core Principles

### 1. Legibility vs Readability
- **Legibility**: How easy it is to distinguish individual letters and characters
- **Readability**: How easy it is to read and understand blocks of text

### 2. Typeface Classifications
- **Serif**: Fonts with small decorative strokes (serifs) at the ends of letterforms
  - Examples: Times New Roman, Georgia, Garamond
  - Best for: Printed materials, long-form reading
- **Sans-serif**: Fonts without serifs
  - Examples: Helvetica, Arial, Roboto, Open Sans
  - Best for: Digital interfaces, modern designs, headings
- **Display/Decorative**: Ornate fonts designed for specific purposes
  - Examples: Script, Blackletter, Novelty fonts
  - Best for: Logos, headlines, special occasions (use sparingly)
- **Monospace**: Fonts where each character occupies the same horizontal space
  - Examples: Courier New, Consolas, Fira Code
  - Best for: Code, technical displays, tabular data

### 3. Anatomy of Type
Key components that affect readability:
- **Baseline**: The line on which most letters sit
- **X-height**: Height of lowercase letters (excluding ascenders and descenders)
- **Ascender**: Part of lowercase letters that extend above the x-height (b, d, f, h, k, l, t)
- **Descender**: Part of lowercase letters that extend below the baseline (g, j, p, q, y)
- **Counter**: Enclosed or partially enclosed negative space in letters (a, b, d, e, g, o, p, q, s)
- **Serif**: Small finishing strokes at the ends of letterforms
- **Stroke**: Main diagonal or vertical lines in letters
- **Bowl**: Curved stroke that creates an enclosed space (b, d, o, p)
- **Terminal**: End of a stroke that doesn't include a serif
- **Shoulder**: Curved stroke connecting the stem to a bowl (h, m, n)

### 4. Typographic Measurements
- **Point Size**: Standard unit for measuring type (1 point = 1/72 inch)
- **Pica**: Unit of measurement equal to 12 points
- **Leading**: Vertical space between lines of text (pronounced "ledding")
- **Tracking**: Uniform spacing across a range of characters
- **Kerning**: Adjustment of space between specific character pairs
- **Measure**: Width of a text block (line length)

## Best Practices

### 1. Hierarchy and Contrast
- Use size, weight, and style to create visual hierarchy
- Establish clear relationships between headings, subheadings, and body text
- Ensure sufficient contrast between text and background

### 2. Alignment and Spacing
- Choose appropriate alignment (left, right, centered, justified) based on context
- Maintain consistent spacing and alignment throughout designs
- Use whitespace effectively to improve readability

### 3. Consistency
- Limit typeface families to 2-3 per design
- Establish and follow a typographic scale
- Use consistent styles for similar elements

### 4. Context Appropriateness
- Match typography to brand personality and tone
- Consider cultural associations of different typefaces
- Adapt typography for different mediums (print vs digital)
